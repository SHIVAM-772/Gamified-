Lines and Angles

The chapter Lines and Angles deals with the relationships between different types of lines and angles formed when two lines intersect or when a transversal cuts two or more lines.

Key Concepts

  • Line: A straight path that extends infinitely in both directions.
  • Line Segment: A part of a line with two fixed end points.
  • Ray: A part of a line that starts from a point and extends infinitely in one direction.
  • Angle: Formed when two rays meet at a common endpoint (vertex).

Types of Angles

  • Acute Angle: Less than 90°
  • Right Angle: Exactly 90°
  • Obtuse Angle: Greater than 90° but less than 180°
  • Straight Angle: Exactly 180°
  • Reflex Angle: Greater than 180° but less than 360°

Important Angle Pairs

  • Complementary Angles: Two angles whose sum is 90°.
  • Supplementary Angles: Two angles whose sum is 180°.
  • Vertically Opposite Angles: Angles opposite to each other when two lines intersect.
  • Adjacent Angles: Angles that share a common arm and vertex.
  • Linear Pair: Two adjacent angles whose non-common arms form a straight line (sum = 180°).

Important Theorems

  • Vertically Opposite Angles Theorem: When two lines intersect, the vertically opposite angles are equal.
  • Angles on a Straight Line: The sum of angles on a straight line is 180°.
  • Angles in a Triangle: The sum of the three interior angles of a triangle is 180°.
  • If a transversal cuts two parallel lines:
    • Corresponding angles are equal.
    • Alternate interior angles are equal.
    • Interior angles on the same side of the transversal are supplementary.

Examples

  1. If two angles are supplementary and one angle is 70°, find the other angle.
    Solution: 180° − 70° = 110°

  2. Two lines intersect and form one angle of 45°. Find all the other angles.
    Solution: Vertically opposite angles = 45°. Adjacent angles = 180° − 45° = 135°
